Namghar (within Vaishnavite Sattras of Majuli)

Confidence: 90%

Description

The Namghar (Assamese: নামঘৰ, literally 'prayer house') is a distinct congregational worship place and central cultural entity associated with the Assamese community, particularly adherents of the Ekasarana Dharma, a monotheistic sect of Hinduism. Within Vaishnavite Sattras of Majuli, it occupies a central position. Architecturally, Namghars are typically simple structures, traditionally constructed using local, eco-friendly materials like bamboo, wood, and thatch, and are notable for the absence of idols or elaborate decorations. The focal point of devotion is the Manikut (or Bhaj-ghar), a dedicated chamber where sacred texts such as the Bhagavata Purana or Kirtan Ghoxa are reverently placed on a seven-tiered wooden platform called the Guru Asana or Singhasana. The design is often considered symbolic of Lord Vishnu's footprint.

Cultural Significance

The Namghar is a cornerstone of Assamese society, serving as a convergence point for spiritual, cultural, and social life that extends beyond religious worship. It functions as a vibrant, inclusive cultural and social hub for the local community in Majuli, open to all individuals regardless of caste, creed, or gender. Namghars facilitate various community activities, including educational programs, political discussions, developmental initiatives, and historically and presently serve as a village public hall, village stage for traditional performances like bhaona, meeting place, and even a village panchayat or trial court for settling local disputes. It also plays a crucial role in grooming individuals in cultural, spiritual, and political leadership.

Historical Context

The origins of the Namghar are deeply rooted in the Neo-Vaishnavite movement of the 15th and 16th centuries, spearheaded by the Assamese saint and social reformer, Srimanta Sankardeva, founder of the Ekasarana Dharma. Sankardeva established the first Namghar at Bordowa in the Nagaon district, introducing them as community spaces for 'naam' (devotional songs) and 'Bhakti' (devotion). Majuli emerged as a prominent cultural and religious center in the 16th century following Sankardeva's visit. A pivotal event, 'Manikanchan Sanjog,' occurred in Majuli when Sankardeva met his foremost disciple, Madhavdeva, which accelerated the Neo-Vaishnavite movement. Throughout the 15th, 16th, and 17th centuries, Sankardeva, Madhavdeva, Damodardev, and other disciples like Vamsigopaladeva were instrumental in establishing numerous Sattras and Namghars across Assam.

Majuli Muga Silk Saree

Description

The Majuli Muga Silk Saree is a handwoven textile from Majuli, Assam, India, crafted from Muga silk, a wild silk variety indigenous to the region. This silk is produced by the silkworm *Antheraea assamensis*, which feeds on Som (*Machilus bombycina*) and Soalu (*Litsea polyantha*) trees. It is characterized by its natural golden-yellow hue, distinctive shimmering, glossy texture that intensifies with washing, and is considered one of the strongest natural silks known for extreme durability, resilience, and stain-resistance. The production process is labor-intensive, requiring approximately 4500 cocoons for one kilogram of raw silk, with a single saree needing about 1000 grams and taking weeks or months to complete. Traditional handlooms are used, and designs often incorporate intricate Assamese motifs such as the Japi, Miri Gos Butta, Paro, Kinkhap, and Kolka.

Cultural Significance

Muga silk holds profound cultural significance in Majuli and across Assam, serving as a powerful symbol of heritage, artistry, and sustainable craftsmanship, deeply embedded in the cultural identity of the Assamese people. It is considered a symbol of purity, status, and wealth, traditionally reserved for royalty and nobility. Owning a Muga mekhela chador is a source of pride, frequently passed down as an heirloom or presented as a gift to brides during weddings. The sarees are integral to significant cultural celebrations, being worn during auspicious occasions, religious ceremonies, and festivals like Bihu. Men also wear Muga silk in the form of dhotis, kurtas, or gamosas during these festivities.

Historical Context

The history of Muga silk weaving in Assam is ancient, tracing back over a thousand years. References to a golden silk from ancient Assam, potentially Muga, appear in the writings of Kautilya from the 4th-3rd century BCE, who mentioned 'Dukula'. Sericulture in Assam has been practiced since time immemorial. The Ahom dynasty (1228 to 1826 AD) is regarded as the 'golden period' for Muga culture, flourishing under royal patronage. During this era, Muga silk was exclusive to royalty, high officials, and aristocrats, with Ahom queens involved in training weavers, and the fabric often gifted to foreign dignitaries. The 7th-century Sanskrit text *Harshacharita* also references valuable fabrics from Kamarupa (ancient Assam). Muga silk received a Geographical Indication (GI) tag for Assam in 2004 and was officially recognized as protected in 2007.

Costumes traditionally feature Assamese silk like Muga and Pat silk, with male dancers wearing dhoti, chadar, and paguri (turban), and female dancers adorning ghuri, chadar, and kanchi (waist cloth), complemented by traditional Assamese jewelry crafted in Kesa Sun (raw gold). Musical accompaniment includes traditional Assamese music, notably Borgeets, played on instruments like the khol (a two-faced drum), taal (cymbals), and flute; modern performances may also include violins and harmoniums.

Cultural Significance

Sattriya Dance is a cornerstone of Assamese cultural heritage, deeply intertwined with the Vaishnavite tradition. The Vaishnavite monasteries (Satras) in Majuli, where it originated, served as vital religious, cultural, and social hubs, meticulously nurturing and preserving this dance form for centuries. Initially, Sattriya was performed exclusively by male monks, known as bhokots, within the monasteries as part of daily rituals and special festivals. Over time, particularly from the mid-20th century, it transitioned from the confines of the Satras to the proscenium stage, with female dancers also embracing the art form. It plays a crucial role in preserving and promoting Assamese identity, fostering a sense of pride among the local community.

Historical Context

Sattriya Dance traces its origins to the 15th century AD, introduced by the great Vaishnava saint and reformer of Assam, Mahapurusha Srimanta Sankardeva. Sankardeva systematized this dance form, drawing inspiration from ancient Indian drama and music texts, particularly Bharata Muni's Nātya Śāstra. His primary motivation was to spread the Vaishnava faith and the philosophy of ek sharan naama dharma (chanting the name of one God devotedly) through an accessible and enjoyable art form. The dance initially developed as an integral part of Ankia Naats (one-act plays) and Bhaona, which were also conceived and written by Sankardeva and his disciple Madhavdeva. Over centuries, the Satras, predominantly built in Majuli, served as the guardians of this sacred dance drama, protecting it from external influences. The dance form also absorbed elements from pre-existing Assamese folk dances such as Ojapali, Devadasi, Bihu, and Bodos. On November 15, 2000, the Sangeet Natak Akademi officially recognized Sattriya as a classical dance of India.
